# Conditioning & Physical Training for Tennis

Tennis is one of the most physically demanding racket sports. A typical competitive singles match lasts 1-2.5 hours, involves 300-600 directional changes, and requires repeated bursts of high-intensity effort with short rest intervals. At the 4.0→4.5 level, physical conditioning becomes a decisive factor — your strokes may be solid, but can you execute them at the same quality in the third set as the first?

This section covers the complete off-court physical training program for competitive tennis, separate from the serve-specific training covered in Training & Drills.

---

## 1. Energy Systems for Tennis

Understanding how tennis uses energy helps you train the right systems.

### The Three Energy Systems

| System | Duration | Tennis Application | How to Train |
|---|---|---|---|
| ATP-CP (Phosphagen) | 0-10 seconds | Individual points, explosive first steps, serves | Short sprints, plyometrics, heavy lifts |
| Glycolytic (Anaerobic) | 10-90 seconds | Extended rallies, tiebreaks under fatigue | Interval training, court sprints, repeated rally drills |
| Aerobic (Oxidative) | 90+ seconds | Recovery between points, match endurance | Steady-state cardio, tempo runs, long rally sets |

### Tennis Work-to-Rest Ratio

During match play, the average point lasts 5-10 seconds with 20-25 seconds rest between points and 90 seconds at changeovers. This creates a roughly **1:2 to 1:5 work-to-rest ratio** — meaning your interval training should mirror these patterns.

**Key insight:** Tennis is primarily an ATP-CP and aerobic sport, not a glycolytic one. You need explosive power (ATP-CP) and the aerobic base to recover between points. Pure anaerobic capacity matters less than the ability to repeat explosive efforts with quick recovery.

## 2. Cardiovascular Endurance

### Aerobic Base Training

Your aerobic base determines how quickly you recover between points and how well you maintain shot quality as a match progresses. Without it, your technique degrades under fatigue — and that's where matches are lost at the 4.0 level.

**Target:** Maintain a heart rate of 65-75% of max for 30-45 minutes, 2-3 times per week.

**Best modalities for tennis:**
- **Running** — most sport-specific, trains the legs under load
- **Cycling** — lower impact, good for recovery days or knee issues
- **Jump rope** — dual purpose: cardio + footwork coordination
- **Swimming** — excellent active recovery, zero impact

### Resting Heart Rate as a Fitness Marker

Track your resting heart rate (first thing in the morning, before getting out of bed). As your aerobic fitness improves, it will trend downward.

| Fitness Level | Resting HR (beats/min) |
|---|---|
| Below average | 75-85 |
| Average | 65-75 |
| Good | 55-65 |
| Excellent (competitive athlete) | 45-55 |

A drop of 5-10 bpm over 8-12 weeks of consistent aerobic training is typical and meaningful.

### Interval Training (Tennis-Specific)

Interval training mimics the start-stop nature of tennis better than steady-state cardio alone.

**Protocol 1: Court Sprints (on court)**
- Sprint baseline to net (12m), jog back. Repeat 10x.
- Rest 60 sec. Do 3 sets.
- Mirrors the explosive-effort-then-recover pattern of match play.

**Protocol 2: 30/30 Intervals (running or cycling)**
- 30 seconds at 85-90% max effort
- 30 seconds easy recovery
- Repeat for 15-20 minutes
- This trains the ATP-CP system with aerobic recovery — exactly what tennis demands.

**Protocol 3: Fartlek Run (unstructured)**
- 25-35 minute run mixing speeds randomly: jog, stride, sprint, walk
- Develops the ability to change gears — critical for match-play unpredictability
- More enjoyable and mentally engaging than structured intervals

## 3. Strength Training

### Why Strength Matters for Tennis

Strength training for tennis isn't about getting big — it's about producing more force, faster, and absorbing the thousands of deceleration forces your body experiences per match. Research shows that a well-designed strength program can increase serve velocity by 5-10%, improve sprint speed to the ball, and significantly reduce injury rates.

### Full-Body Program (2x/week)

This program complements the serve-specific shoulder and core work covered in Training & Drills. Schedule strength sessions on non-court days or at least 4 hours separated from court sessions.

#### Lower Body (Power & Stability)

| Exercise | Sets x Reps | Tennis Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Barbell squat or goblet squat | 3 x 8-10 | Leg drive for serves, groundstrokes, and court coverage |
| Romanian deadlift | 3 x 8-10 | Posterior chain strength, deceleration ability |
| Bulgarian split squat | 3 x 8/leg | Single-leg stability, mimics lunge-to-ball movement |
| Lateral lunge | 3 x 8/side | Lateral loading — directly mirrors court movement |
| Calf raises (slow eccentric) | 3 x 15 | Achilles tendon resilience, split step loading |

#### Upper Body (Functional Strength)

| Exercise | Sets x Reps | Tennis Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Push-ups or dumbbell bench press | 3 x 10-12 | Chest and shoulder stability for serve and overhead |
| Dumbbell row | 3 x 10/arm | Back strength, scapular control, injury prevention |
| Overhead press | 3 x 8-10 | Shoulder strength through full range of motion |
| Face pulls (band or cable) | 3 x 15 | Posterior shoulder health — counteracts serve and forehand dominance |
| Farmer's carry | 3 x 30m | Grip endurance, trunk stability, general durability |

#### Core (Anti-Rotation & Stability)

| Exercise | Sets x Reps | Tennis Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Pallof press | 3 x 10/side | Anti-rotation: resisting trunk twist under load |
| Dead bug | 3 x 10/side | Core stability with limb movement — mimics stroke mechanics |
| Side plank | 3 x 30 sec/side | Lateral core endurance for open-stance shots |
| Woodchop (cable or band) | 3 x 10/side | Rotational power transfer |
| Plank with shoulder tap | 3 x 10/side | Stability under dynamic load |

### Strength Periodization

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Reps/Sets |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypertrophy/Base | 4-6 weeks (off-season) | Muscle endurance, joint prep | 3 x 12-15, moderate weight |
| Strength | 4-6 weeks (pre-season) | Maximum force production | 4 x 5-8, heavier weight |
| Power | 4-6 weeks (in-season start) | Speed of force application | 3 x 3-5, explosive, lighter weight |
| Maintenance | Season duration | Preserve gains, prevent injury | 2 x 6-8, moderate weight, 1-2x/week |

<div class="feel-cue">
<strong>What It Feels Like</strong>
When your strength base is solid, you'll notice it most in the second half of matches. Shots that used to feel heavy — the deep ball you have to dig out, the wide forehand at full stretch — feel manageable because your legs and core aren't fatigued. You hit your 40th forehand of the match with the same racket head speed as your 5th.

## 4. Speed & Agility

### Linear Speed

Tennis rarely requires long sprints. The average distance covered in a single point is 8-15 meters, with the critical first 3 steps covering about 3-4 meters. First-step quickness matters more than top-end speed.

**First-Step Drills:**
- Reaction sprints from various starting positions (facing away, seated, lying down): 3 x 6
- 5-meter sprint starts with split step trigger: 3 x 8
- Falling start sprints (lean forward until you must step): 3 x 6

**Acceleration:**
- 10-meter sprints from standing: 6-8 reps, full recovery between
- Resisted sprints (partner holding band around waist): 3 x 6

### Change of Direction

Tennis demands constant direction changes — 4-6 per point on average. The ability to decelerate, plant, and re-accelerate is the athletic quality that separates levels.

**Key drills:**
- Pro agility (5-10-5) shuttle: 3 x 4 reps
- T-drill: 3 x 3 reps
- Lateral shuffle to sprint transitions: 3 x 6 reps
- 180-degree turn and sprint: 3 x 6 reps

**Deceleration training** is often neglected but critical for injury prevention:
- Approach sprint with controlled 2-step stop: 3 x 8
- Lateral bound with stick landing (hold 2 sec): 3 x 6/side
- Drop step and backpedal to controlled stop: 3 x 6

## 5. Flexibility & Mobility

### Dynamic Warm-Up (Pre-Court, 10-12 min)

Perform these before every court session. Static stretching before playing reduces power output — save it for after.

1. Light jog (2 min)
2. High knees (20m)
3. Butt kicks (20m)
4. Lateral shuffles (20m each direction)
5. Carioca / grapevine (20m each direction)
6. Walking lunges with rotation (10/side)
7. Leg swings — forward/back, side to side (10 each leg)
8. Arm circles — small to large (20 each direction)
9. Hip circles (10 each direction)
10. Trunk rotations (10 each direction)

### Static Stretching (Post-Court, 10-15 min)

Hold each stretch 30-45 seconds. Breathe through the stretch — don't force range of motion.

| Stretch | Target | Tennis Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Standing quad stretch | Quadriceps | Court coverage, lunging |
| Standing hamstring stretch (foot on bench) | Hamstrings | Forward reach, low balls |
| Hip flexor lunge | Hip flexors, psoas | Open-stance shots, serve leg drive |
| Pigeon pose or figure-4 | Glutes, external rotators | Rotation mechanics, low shots |
| Cross-body shoulder stretch | Posterior shoulder | Serve deceleration muscles |
| Sleeper stretch | Internal rotation | Serves and overhead — non-negotiable |
| Doorway chest stretch | Pectorals, anterior shoulder | Counteracts forward-rounded posture |
| Seated spinal twist | Thoracic spine | Trunk rotation range |
| Calf stretch (wall) | Gastrocnemius, soleus | Split step, push-off mechanics |

### Key Mobility Areas for Tennis Players

**Thoracic spine rotation** — If your upper back is stiff, your shoulder and lower back compensate. This increases injury risk on serves and forehands. Target: 45+ degrees of rotation each direction.

**Hip internal rotation** — Critical for loading in open-stance shots. If your hips are restricted, your knees absorb the rotational force instead. Target: 35+ degrees each hip.

**Ankle dorsiflexion** — Determines how deep you can load your split step and how well you can decelerate. Target: knee 4+ inches past toes in a wall ankle test.

## 6. Match Fitness & Endurance

### Building Match Stamina

The best way to build match-specific endurance is progressive match play. But you can supplement with these structured sessions:

**On-Court Endurance Sets:**
- Rally 15 balls cross-court, 15 balls down-the-line, no breaks between switches. 3 x through.
- Point play with no sit-downs: play a full set without resting at changeovers.
- Serve 20 balls, immediately play a point, serve 20 more, play a point. Continue for 15 minutes.

**Simulated Match Fatigue:**
- Run 400m (or 1 min of court sprints), then immediately play a 3-point game. Repeat 6x.
- This trains shot quality under cardiovascular stress — the exact scenario where 4.0 players break down.

### Heat Acclimatization

If you play outdoors in warm climates:
- Acclimatize gradually over 10-14 days of increasing heat exposure
- Pre-hydrate: 16-20 oz of water 2 hours before play
- During play: 4-8 oz every changeover, with electrolytes if sweating heavily
- Post-play: replace 150% of fluid lost (weigh before and after to estimate)
- Wear light-colored, moisture-wicking clothing
- Use ice towels at changeovers on neck and wrists

## 7. Weekly Training Template

### In-Season (Playing 2-3 matches/week)

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Monday | Strength training (full body, moderate intensity) |
| Tuesday | Court practice (1.5 hrs) + dynamic warm-up |
| Wednesday | Light cardio (30 min jog or bike) + flexibility work |
| Thursday | Court practice or match + dynamic warm-up |
| Friday | Strength training (lighter, maintenance) + agility drills |
| Saturday | Match day — dynamic warm-up, post-match stretching |
| Sunday | Active recovery: walk, swim, yoga, or full rest |

### Off-Season (No competitive matches)

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Monday | Strength training (heavy, building phase) |
| Tuesday | Interval training (30/30s or fartlek) + agility drills |
| Wednesday | Court practice (technique focus) + flexibility |
| Thursday | Strength training (full body) |
| Friday | Court practice + on-court conditioning sets |
| Saturday | Long aerobic session (45-60 min run, bike, or swim) |
| Sunday | Active recovery or full rest |

### Training Load Management

Listen to your body. These signs indicate you need to reduce load:

- Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above normal for 2+ consecutive mornings
- Persistent muscle soreness that doesn't resolve with a rest day
- Sleep quality declining despite consistent habits
- Decreased motivation or enjoyment of play
- Recurrent minor injuries (tweaks, strains)

**The 10% rule:** Don't increase weekly training volume (total hours or total intensity) by more than 10% week over week. Rapid ramp-ups are the primary cause of overuse injuries in recreational athletes.

## 8. Nutrition for Tennis Performance

### Pre-Match Nutrition

| Timing | What to Eat | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 3-4 hours before | Full meal: complex carbs + lean protein + vegetables | Tops off glycogen, allows full digestion |
| 1-2 hours before | Light snack: banana + peanut butter, or granola bar | Quick energy without stomach issues |
| 30 min before | Small piece of fruit or sports drink | Immediate glucose availability |

**Avoid:** High-fiber foods, heavy fats, or anything new/untested before a match.

### During Match

- **Water:** 4-8 oz every changeover
- **Electrolytes:** Add to water if match exceeds 1 hour or in heat
- **Quick carbs:** Banana, dates, or energy chews at every other changeover for matches over 90 min
- **Caffeine:** 100-200mg (one coffee) 30-60 min before play can improve reaction time and reduce perceived effort — but test in practice first, not matches

### Post-Match Recovery Nutrition

- **Within 30 minutes:** 20-30g protein + 40-60g carbs (shake + banana, or chocolate milk)
- **Within 2 hours:** Full balanced meal
- **Key micronutrients:** Magnesium (prevents cramping), vitamin D (bone and immune health), omega-3s (inflammation management)

### Hydration Guidelines

| Body Weight | Minimum Daily Water | During Play (per hour) |
|---|---|---|
| 140 lbs | 70 oz (2.1L) | 16-24 oz |
| 170 lbs | 85 oz (2.5L) | 20-28 oz |
| 200 lbs | 100 oz (3.0L) | 24-32 oz |

**Hydration test:** Your urine should be pale yellow. Dark yellow = dehydrated. Clear = overhydrated (rare but possible with excessive intake).

<strong>What It Feels Like</strong>
When your conditioning is right, you stop worrying about fitness during matches. You don't dread the third set. You notice your opponent slowing down while you feel the same as you did in the first game. The mental benefit is as big as the physical one — confidence that you can outlast anyone at your level frees you to play aggressively instead of conservatively.
