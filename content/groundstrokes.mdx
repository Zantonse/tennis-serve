# Groundstroke Technique: Forehand & Backhand

Groundstrokes are the shots you hit most in tennis. In a typical match, forehands and backhands account for 70-80% of all shots played. At the 4.0 level, your groundstrokes are functional — you can rally and direct the ball. At 4.5, they need to be *weapons*: consistent under pressure, capable of generating both depth and angle, and able to shift between offense and defense within a rally.

---

## 1. The Forehand

### Setup and Preparation

**Grip:** Semi-Western (Bevel 4) for most players. See the Grip Guide for alternatives.

**Ready position → Unit turn:**
1. See the ball coming to your forehand side
2. Turn your shoulders and hips together (the unit turn)
3. Non-dominant hand stays on the racket throat, guiding it back
4. Weight shifts to the outside foot (right foot for right-handers)
5. Racket arm relaxes — let gravity and the turn take the racket back

**The backswing should be compact.** A common 4.0 error is a long, looping backswing that takes too long to complete. The racket doesn't need to go behind your body — it just needs to reach the "slot" position where the butt cap points roughly toward the incoming ball.

### The Swing Path

**Low-to-high is the fundamental forehand swing path.**

1. **Drop:** The racket drops below the incoming ball (the "racket drop"). With a Semi-Western grip, this happens naturally as the arm relaxes.
2. **Forward swing:** The hips fire first, the trunk follows, the arm lags behind, then whips through the contact zone.
3. **Contact:** Ball meets strings slightly in front of the front hip, at approximately waist height. The racket face is roughly perpendicular to the ground or slightly closed.
4. **Windshield wiper finish:** After contact, the forearm pronates and the racket sweeps across the body. The racket finishes over the opposite shoulder or wraps past it.

### Contact Point

The contact point is the single most important factor in forehand consistency.

| Contact Position | Result | When It Happens |
|---|---|---|
| In front of front hip | Clean, powerful, directed | Correct timing and footwork |
| Even with the body | Less power, less control | Slightly late preparation |
| Behind the body | Weak, off-direction, injury risk | Late reaction, poor footwork |
| Too far from the body | Arm-only shot, no chain | Poor lateral positioning |
| Too close to the body | Jammed, cramped swing | Didn't adjust spacing |

**The test:** If you can see the ball and your racket at the same time at the moment of contact (peripheral vision), you're hitting in front. If you have to turn your head to see the racket, you're hitting late.

### Forehand Targets and Patterns

| Pattern | Direction | When to Use | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cross-court (inside-out) | Forehand to forehand | Default rally pattern — highest margin | Low |
| Inside-out forehand | Run around backhand, hit to opponent's backhand | Attacking with your weapon | Medium |
| Down-the-line | Straight ahead | Change of direction, passing shot | High |
| Short angle cross-court | Sharp cross-court, lands near service line | Pull opponent off court | Medium-High |

**The 4.5 forehand pattern:** Cross-court until you get a short ball or a weak reply, then either go down-the-line or inside-out to the open court. The forehand is your weapon — construct points to use it.

### Common Forehand Errors (4.0 Level)

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ball goes long consistently | Open racket face, no topspin | Steeper low-to-high path; windshield wiper finish |
| Ball goes into the net | Hitting late, racket face too closed | Hit further in front; check grip isn't too extreme |
| No power despite full swing | Arm-only swing, no kinetic chain | Initiate with hip rotation; feel the legs |
| Inconsistent direction | Variable contact point | Better footwork to get to the same position every time |
| Shanks and mishits | Eyes leave the ball | Watch the ball to contact; head stays still |
| Heavy arm after practice | Death grip, muscling the ball | Lighter grip pressure (3-4/10); let the body do the work |

## 2. The Backhand

### Two-Handed Backhand

**Grip:** Bottom hand Continental (Bevel 2), Top hand Eastern or Semi-Western forehand (Bevel 3/4).

**Setup and Preparation:**
1. Recognize the ball to the backhand side
2. Unit turn — both hands on the racket, turn shoulders and hips together
3. The non-dominant arm (top hand) drives the racket back
4. Backswing is compact — shorter than the forehand
5. Weight shifts to the front foot

**The Swing:**
1. **Forward swing:** The top hand pushes the racket forward while the hips open. Think of it as hitting a forehand with your non-dominant hand.
2. **Contact:** In front of the front hip, both arms slightly bent, racket face square to the target.
3. **Follow-through:** Both arms extend through the ball, then the racket rises over the dominant shoulder.

**Two-handed advantages:**
- Extra stability from the second hand (more forgiving on off-center hits)
- Easier to handle heavy pace (more effective mass)
- Easier to learn and develop consistency

**Two-handed limitations:**
- Less reach (arms stay closer to the body)
- Harder to generate angles (shorter lever arm)
- Must develop a one-handed slice for variety

### One-Handed Backhand

**Grip:** Eastern Backhand (Bevel 1).

**Setup and Preparation:**
1. Unit turn — bigger shoulder rotation than the two-hander (90-110 degrees)
2. Non-dominant hand cradles the racket at the throat during the turn
3. Non-dominant hand releases as the body coils
4. The hitting arm extends behind the body with the racket tip up

**The Swing:**
1. **Forward swing:** Led by the shoulder, the arm extends forward. Keep the arm relatively straight through contact.
2. **Contact:** Arm nearly fully extended, in front of the front foot. The wrist is laid back (dorsiflexed).
3. **Follow-through:** The arm continues up and across, finishing high over the non-dominant shoulder.

**One-handed advantages:**
- Greater reach (extended arm)
- More natural slice backhand
- Better angle generation (longer lever arm)
- Aesthetically beautiful when grooved

**One-handed limitations:**
- Smaller timing window (must be precise with contact point)
- Harder to handle heavy topspin (single arm absorbs force)
- Takes longer to develop consistency

### Which Backhand to Use?

If you're at 4.0 and still deciding: **the two-handed backhand is easier to develop to 4.5.** The timing is more forgiving, it handles pace better, and consistency comes faster. The one-handed backhand has a higher ceiling aesthetically and in terms of angles, but a steeper learning curve.

If you already have one, stick with it — switching backhands at this level costs more time than improving the one you have.

### The Slice Backhand (Both Styles)

Every player needs a slice backhand, regardless of whether their primary backhand is one-handed or two-handed.

**Grip:** Continental (Bevel 2).

**Swing path:** High-to-low. The racket starts above the ball and swings forward and slightly downward through contact. The racket face is slightly open (tilted upward).

**Uses:**
- Approach shot (the natural forward weight transfer suits net approaches)
- Defensive return of serve (compact, reliable)
- Change of pace (takes speed off the ball, disrupts opponent's rhythm)
- Low ball handling (easier to get under balls below knee height)
- Drop shot setup (similar motion disguises the drop shot)

**Common error:** Chopping down on the ball (too steep, ball pops up). The slice should travel forward with a slight downward angle — think "slice through the ball," not "chop down on it."

| Slice Quality | Swing Direction | Ball Flight |
|---|---|---|
| Good slice | Forward and slightly down | Low, skidding, stays low after bounce |
| Floater (bad) | Steeply downward | Pops up, sits up for opponent to attack |
| Knifing slice (advanced) | Forward with slight side angle | Curves away from opponent, stays very low |

## 3. Depth and Height: The Two Controls

### Net Clearance

The height over the net determines the depth and safety of your groundstroke.

| Net Clearance | Shot Type | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 feet | Flat drive | Attacking a short ball, going for a winner |
| 3-4 feet | Standard topspin rally ball | Default for cross-court rallying |
| 5-7 feet | Heavy topspin | Pushing opponent deep, resetting the point |
| 8+ feet | Defensive lob | When pulled way out of position |

**The 4.0 error:** Hitting everything 1-2 feet over the net. This produces flashy shots when they go in, but a high error rate. **The 4.5 upgrade:** Clearing the net by 3-4 feet with topspin as the default. The topspin brings the ball down into the court, giving you margin while maintaining depth.

### Depth Targets

| Depth | Court Position | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Deep (within 4 feet of baseline) | Back of the court | Default rally ball — keeps opponent pinned back |
| Mid-court | Between service line and baseline | Transition balls (careful — these get attacked) |
| Short (inside service line) | Front of court | Drop shots, angled winners only |

**Rule of thumb:** If you're not sure where to hit, hit deep cross-court. This is the highest-percentage shot in tennis at every level.

## 4. Rally Construction

### The 3-Shot Pattern

Most points at the 4.0-4.5 level are decided within 3-5 shots. The 3-shot pattern is:

1. **Neutral ball:** Deep cross-court, 3-4 feet over the net with topspin. Build the rally.
2. **Opportunity ball:** Opponent hits short or weak. Move forward, hit with more pace or to the open court.
3. **Finishing ball:** Open court is exposed. Hit to the open space or approach the net.

**The mistake:** Trying to hit a finishing ball on every shot. At 4.0, this produces exciting points with lots of winners *and* lots of unforced errors. At 4.5, you construct the point and wait for the opportunity.

### Forehand vs. Backhand: Play to Your Strength

| Situation | 4.0 Approach | 4.5 Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Ball to your forehand | Hit hard, go for winner | Rally deep cross-court, wait for opportunity |
| Ball to your backhand | Just get it back | Direct it cross-court with depth, look for forehand next |
| Short ball | Hit hard at it | Step in, direct to open court, approach net |
| Opponent's weak side identified | Occasionally exploit it | Systematically target it with every pattern |

### The Inside-Out Forehand

The most important offensive pattern at the 4.5 level. When a ball comes to your backhand side but isn't too wide:

1. Move around the ball to hit a forehand instead
2. Hit inside-out (cross-court from the backhand side to the opponent's backhand)
3. This uses your strongest shot to attack their likely weaker side

**When to use it:** When you're positioned within 2-3 feet of center and the ball isn't pulling you too wide. If the ball is genuinely wide to your backhand, hit the backhand — don't chase the forehand.

## 5. Practice Drills for Groundstrokes

### Consistency Building

**Cross-Court 20:** Rally cross-court with a partner, forehand to forehand. Count consecutive balls. Goal: 20 in a row without an error. Then switch to backhand cross-court.

**Deep Zone Rally:** Place targets (cones or towels) 4 feet inside the baseline. Rally and count how many balls land in the deep zone. Goal: 60% or more.

### Control and Direction

**Figure-8 Drill:** Rally cross-court for 3 balls, then down-the-line for 1, then cross-court for 3, then down-the-line for 1. This trains directional changes with a controlled pattern.

**Target Practice:** Place 4 targets on the court (deep cross-court, short cross-court, deep down-the-line, short down-the-line). Partner feeds balls; call out the target before you hit. 3 x 20 feeds.

### Power Development

**Speed Ladder Rally:** Rally at 50% pace for 10 balls, then 70% for 10, then 85% for 10. Focus on maintaining the same technique as you add pace. If errors increase, drop the pace back.

**Short-Court to Full-Court:** Start rallying from the service line (short court). After 10 balls, both players step back to no-man's land. After 10 more, step back to the baseline. This builds feel before adding power.

### Pressure Simulation

**First-Strike Drill:** Partner feeds a medium ball. You must hit an aggressive shot (not a winner — just with pace and direction) within the first 2 balls. Teaches attacking off the return and early rally ball.

**21-Point Game:** Play cross-court only. First to 21 wins. Every ball must land past the service line. This builds consistency under competitive pressure.

<div class="feel-cue">
<strong>What It Feels Like</strong>
A well-struck groundstroke has a distinctive feel: the ball meets the sweet spot, the racket doesn't twist in your hand, and the ball jumps off the strings with a clean pop. There's no vibration, no jarring impact. Your arm barely registers the hit because the body weight behind the shot absorbed the force. When your groundstrokes are working, rallying feels effortless — you're just redirecting the ball's energy rather than generating everything yourself.
