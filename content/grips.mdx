# Grip Guide: Every Grip for Every Shot

Your grip is the only connection between your body and the racket. Changing your grip by a single bevel changes the angle of the racket face at contact, which changes the spin, trajectory, and power of every shot. At the 4.0 level, most players use one or two grips for everything. At 4.5, you need fluid grip changes between shots — and the right grip for each situation.

---

## 1. The Bevel System

A tennis racket handle has 8 bevels (flat sides), numbered 1-8. The reference point is the **base knuckle of your index finger** — where it sits on the handle determines your grip.

### Bevel Map (Right-Handed Player)

Hold the racket in front of you with the strings facing the side fence. The top bevel (facing up) is Bevel 1.

| Bevel | Position |
|---|---|
| 1 | Top (12 o'clock) |
| 2 | Upper right (1:30) |
| 3 | Right (3 o'clock) |
| 4 | Lower right (4:30) |
| 5 | Bottom (6 o'clock) |
| 6 | Lower left (7:30) |
| 7 | Left (9 o'clock) |
| 8 | Upper left (10:30) |

*Left-handed players: mirror the numbers (Bevel 2 becomes upper left, etc.)*

## 2. The Grips

### Continental (Bevel 2)

**The "hammer grip" — base knuckle on Bevel 2.**

Hold the racket like a hammer. The V formed by your thumb and index finger sits on top of the handle, slightly toward the right.

| Best For | Why |
|---|---|
| Serve (all types) | Allows pronation for flat, slice, and kick serves |
| Volley (forehand and backhand) | Neutral face angle for quick reactions at net |
| Overhead smash | Same motion as the serve |
| Slice forehand and backhand | Natural open face for underspin |
| Drop shot | Delicate touch with open face |

**At the 4.0→4.5 level:** If you're not using Continental for your serve, this is priority #1. A forehand grip on the serve physically prevents pronation — you'll never develop a kick serve or consistent flat serve without it.

**Common error:** Switching to an Eastern or Semi-Western grip for volleys. This closes the racket face, causing volleys to go into the net. All volleys should use Continental.

### Eastern Forehand (Bevel 3)

**Base knuckle on Bevel 3 — like shaking hands with the racket.**

The racket face is roughly perpendicular to the ground at contact with a neutral swing.

| Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Flat forehands | Natural flat contact, easy power |
| Beginner/developing players | Most intuitive grip to learn |
| Low balls | Easier to get under low bounces |
| Approach shots | Promotes forward weight transfer |

**Topspin potential:** Moderate. You can generate topspin with a low-to-high swing, but the grip limits how much brush you can apply compared to Semi-Western.

**Limitations:** Hard to generate heavy topspin on high-bouncing balls. The contact point is more in front and to the side, which can be awkward on balls above shoulder height.

### Semi-Western Forehand (Bevel 4)

**Base knuckle on Bevel 4 — the modern default.**

The most popular forehand grip on the professional tour. Balances topspin and power.

| Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Topspin forehands | Natural racket face angle promotes topspin |
| High-bouncing balls | Comfortable contact at shoulder height |
| Modern baseline tennis | The standard for 4.0+ play |
| Rally consistency | Topspin provides margin over the net |

**Why this is the 4.5 standard:** Semi-Western naturally closes the racket face slightly, which means a low-to-high swing produces topspin without conscious wrist manipulation. It lets you swing fast with margin.

**Topspin potential:** High. Professionals generate 2,000-3,500 RPM with this grip.

**Limitations:** Harder to hit low balls (the closed face struggles below knee height). Slice and flat shots require more wrist adjustment.

### Western Forehand (Bevel 5)

**Base knuckle on Bevel 5 — under the handle.**

Extreme topspin grip. The racket face is heavily closed, requiring a very steep low-to-high swing.

| Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Heavy topspin (clay court) | Maximum spin potential, 3,000-5,000+ RPM |
| High balls | Very comfortable above shoulder height |
| Defensive lobs | Natural upward trajectory |

**Limitations:** Very difficult to hit low balls, slice, or flat shots. Requires excellent footwork to set up properly. Most recreational players don't need this grip — Semi-Western provides enough topspin for the 4.0-4.5 range.

**Used by:** Nadal (close to full Western), many clay court specialists.

### Eastern Backhand (Bevel 1)

**Base knuckle on Bevel 1 — on top of the handle.**

The standard one-handed backhand grip.

| Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| One-handed topspin backhand | Natural topspin production with extended arm |
| One-handed slice backhand | Slightly open face for slice |
| Drop shots from the backhand side | Delicate touch |

**Note:** For a two-handed backhand, the dominant hand (bottom) typically uses a Continental or slight Eastern backhand grip, while the non-dominant hand (top) uses an Eastern or Semi-Western forehand grip. The top hand drives the shot.

### Two-Handed Backhand Grip

**Bottom hand: Continental (Bevel 2). Top hand: Eastern or Semi-Western forehand (Bevel 3 or 4).**

| Component | Grip | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Bottom hand (dominant) | Continental | Guides direction, provides stability |
| Top hand (non-dominant) | Eastern/Semi-Western FH | Drives the swing, generates power and spin |

**Key insight:** The two-handed backhand is essentially a one-handed forehand hit with the non-dominant hand, supported by the dominant hand. Think of your top hand as the engine and your bottom hand as the steering wheel.

## 4. Grip Changes During Play

### The 4.0 Problem: One Grip for Everything

Many 4.0 players use the same Semi-Western forehand grip for everything — forehands, backhands, volleys, and serves. This works *okay* for forehands but produces:
- Serves with no pronation (weak, flat, predictable)
- Volleys that go into the net (closed face)
- Backhands with no spin or stability
- Slices that pop up (wrong face angle)

### How Grip Changes Work

Grip changes happen in the **non-dominant hand** during preparation, not during the swing. The sequence:

1. Between shots, the racket rests in the non-dominant hand (the "ready" hand)
2. As you recognize the incoming shot, the non-dominant hand rotates the racket slightly
3. The dominant hand finds the new grip position
4. This happens during the unit turn — it's part of preparation, not a separate step

**Practice drill:** Rally with a partner. On every ball, consciously feel your grip change during the unit turn. Forehand → Semi-Western. Backhand → 2H grip or Eastern BH. This feels slow at first but becomes automatic within 2-3 weeks.

### Which Grip Changes Matter Most

| Transition | How Often | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Forehand → Backhand (and back) | Every other shot | #1 — must be automatic |
| Groundstroke → Continental (for volleys) | Every net approach | #2 — critical for net play |
| Forehand → Continental (for serve) | Every service game | #3 — non-negotiable |
| Forehand → Continental (for slice) | Frequent | #4 — expands your shot variety |

## 5. Grip Pressure Revisited

| Phase | Pressure (1-10) | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Ready position | 3-4 | Allows quick grip change |
| Backswing | 3-4 | Maximizes wrist flexibility and racket lag |
| Forward swing | 4-5 | Building toward contact |
| Contact | 6-7 | Stabilizes racket face, transfers force |
| Follow-through | 3-4 | Relaxes for recovery and next grip change |

**The key insight:** Grip pressure should cycle with every shot. A constant death-grip (8-10) reduces racket head speed by 10-20%, causes tennis elbow, and makes grip changes physically impossible because your hand is locked.

### The Finger Pressure Map

Not all fingers contribute equally:

- **Index finger and thumb:** Direction control — they guide the racket face angle
- **Middle finger:** Primary power finger — provides stability at contact
- **Ring and pinky fingers:** Support — prevent the racket from twisting

On a forehand, the index finger knuckle wraps slightly around the bevel (the "trigger finger" position). This gives you fine control over the racket face angle without gripping hard.

<div class="feel-cue">
<strong>What It Feels Like</strong>
The right grip feels natural for the shot you're hitting — you shouldn't be thinking about it during a rally. When your forehand grip is correct, the racket face naturally faces the right direction at contact without wrist manipulation. When your serve grip is correct, pronation happens automatically. If you're fighting the racket face on any shot, your grip is probably wrong. The fix is almost always moving one bevel in the right direction.
