# Tennis Serve: Technical Phases & Serve Types (Detailed)

*Primary source: Kovacs M, Ellenbecker T. "An 8-Stage Model for Evaluating the Tennis Serve." Sports Health. 2011;3(6):504-513. PMCID: PMC3445225. Supporting: FeelTennis.net drone analysis; TennisCompanion.org; UTR Sports (2025); Performance-Plus Tennis ATP/WTA toss analysis (2024).*

## The Governing Framework: 3 Phases, 8 Stages

- **Preparation** (Stages 1-4): Start, Release/Toss, Loading, Cocking
- **Acceleration** (Stages 5-6): Acceleration, Contact
- **Follow-Through** (Stages 7-8): Deceleration, Finish/Landing

Legs and trunk contribute 51-55% of kinetic energy to the hand. A 20% reduction in trunk energy requires a 34% velocity increase or 70% mass increase in the arm to compensate.

---

## Stage 1: Stance & Setup

### Platform Stance (Foot-Back)
- Both feet stay on ground; wider base allows deeper squat
- Front knee flexion: ~65.5 +/- 12.6 degrees
- Lower vertical ground reaction forces than foot-up
- Used by Federer, Djokovic
- Advantage: stability, easier balance post-serve

### Pinpoint Stance (Foot-Up)
- Back foot brought to front foot during loading
- Higher vertical GRF; rear knee angular velocity: 9.3 +/- 1.2 rad/s (vs 7.2 for foot-back)
- Ball velocities NOT significantly different between stances (Bahamonde & Knudson, 2001)
- Larger horizontal braking forces at landing — relevant for serve-and-volley

**Grip:** Continental — base knuckle of index finger on bevel 2. Strings face side fence at trophy position.

---

## Stage 2: The Toss (Release)

- Release from fingertips, not palm
- Tossing arm stays long and slow
- Pro toss heights: Djokovic ~24in, Alcaraz ~24in, Rublev 27+in, Rune ~36in
- Optimal: ball descending slightly at contact

### Toss Placement by Serve Type

| Serve | Position |
|-------|----------|
| Flat | 12-18in front, ~6in right of shoulder |
| Slice | Same as flat, fractionally further right |
| Kick | Directly above head, slightly behind |

**Injury note:** Toss too close to 12 o'clock increases subacromial impingement risk. Must be slightly lateral, facilitating ~100 degree arm abduction at contact.

---

## Stage 3: Loading (Trophy Position)

- Racket elbow ~90 degrees, racket roughly vertical
- Shoulders coiled ~90-110 degrees (FeelTennis drone measured ~110 degrees)
- Pelvis and shoulders show lateral rear tilt
- Front knee flexion >15 degrees recommended
- Spine in hyperextension, ipsilateral lateral flexion, and rotation
- Heel raised on back foot, loading on ball of foot

---

## Stage 4: Cocking (Racket Drop)

### Checkpoint Angles at Maximum External Rotation (Olympic Professionals)

| Measurement | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Shoulder abduction | 101 +/- 13 degrees |
| Horizontal adduction | 7 +/- 13 degrees |
| **External rotation** | **172 +/- 12 degrees** |
| Elbow flexion | 104 +/- 12 degrees |
| Wrist extension | 66 +/- 19 degrees |

Max external rotation reached 0.090 +/- 0.014 seconds before contact.

### Muscle Activation at Cocking Phase

| Muscle | % MVC |
|--------|-------|
| Serratus anterior | 70% |
| Supraspinatus | 53% |
| Infraspinatus | 41% |
| Biceps brachii | 39% |
| Subscapularis | 25% |

The **racket drop / power loop** is the key differentiator between advanced and beginner servers. Beginners shortcut directly upward, missing the energy storage.

---

## Stage 5: Acceleration

**Timing:** Max external rotation to contact in < 10 ms. Vertical force: 1.68-2.12x body weight.

### Uncoiling Sequence (Proximal-to-Distal)
1. Legs extend explosively upward
2. Trunk rotates and flexes (rapid lumbar "corkscrew" reversal)
3. Shoulder horizontally adducts and internally rotates
4. Elbow extends
5. Forearm pronates
6. Wrist snap (minor — mechanism is primarily pronation)

### Upper Body Muscle Activation at Acceleration

| Muscle | % MVC |
|--------|-------|
| **Pectoralis major** | **115%** |
| **Subscapularis** | **113%** |
| Serratus anterior | 74% |
| Latissimus dorsi | 57% |

Supramaximal values (>100% MVC) occur due to the stretch-shortening cycle from Stage 4 loading — this IS the fascial catapult mechanism in action.

---

## Stage 6: Contact

### Body Position at Contact (Olympic Professionals)

| Measurement | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Trunk tilt | 48 +/- 7 degrees above horizontal |
| Arm abduction | 101 degrees (optimal: 110 +/- 15) |
| Elbow flexion | 20 +/- 4 degrees (nearly full extension) |
| Wrist extension | 15 +/- 8 degrees |
| Front knee flexion | 24 +/- 14 degrees |

**Racket head speed:** 38-47 m/s (85-105 mph)

**Pronation timing:** ~0.066 seconds — below conscious control threshold. Must be trained as motor pattern.

### Contact Point by Serve Type

| Serve | Contact Zone | Racket Face | Spin |
|-------|-------------|-------------|------|
| Flat | Center of ball, slightly in front | Perpendicular (flush) | Minimal |
| Slice | Outer/right edge (3 o'clock) | Angled sideways | Sidespin |
| Kick | Bottom-left (7-8 o'clock), behind head | Brushes upward | Heavy topspin |

**Key finding:** Differences between serve types occur ONLY in the upper kinetic chain. Lower body mechanics do not differ significantly (Chow et al 2009, EMG of 8 lower trunk muscles).

---

## Stage 7: Deceleration (Most Violent Phase)

- Deceleration forces: up to 300 N-m between trunk and arm
- Shoulder distraction forces: 0.5-0.75x body weight
- Continued internal rotation after contact is normal and protective
- Posterior rotator cuff: 30-35% MVC; Serratus anterior: 53% MVC

---

## Stage 8: Finish/Landing

- Front foot lands into court
- Large eccentric forces require lower body deceleration training
- Right erector spinae highly active for trunk stabilization
- Recovery: bring racket to ready position, pivot to face net

---

## Serve Types: Detailed Breakdown

### Flat Serve
- **Grip:** True Continental (bevel 2)
- **Toss:** 12-18in front, ~6in right; slightly into court
- **Contact:** Racket face flush; maximum pronation
- **Result:** Low bounce, straight, maximum speed
- **Use:** First serve when ahead; T or wide; target center of net (lowest point)

### Slice Serve
- **Grip:** Continental; micro-rotation toward Eastern BH possible
- **Toss:** Same as flat or fractionally further right
- **Contact:** 3 o'clock on ball; swing path moves right before cutting across
- **Result:** Right-to-left curve (righties); low bounce; effective on grass
- **Use:** Pull opponent wide on deuce; jam body; attack lefties' forehand

### Kick / Topspin Serve
- **Grip:** Continental; micro-rotation toward Eastern BH for topspin leverage (UTR Sports)
- **Toss:** Directly above head, slightly behind; contact behind head (FeelTennis drone confirmed)
- **Contact:** 7-8 o'clock; upward + lateral brushing; more vertical racket path
- **Result:** High net clearance, sharp dip, shoulder-height bounce with lateral kick
- **Use:** Default second serve; attack backhand; clay courts

### Grip Disagreement
TennisCompanion: strict Continental for disguise. UTR Sports: micro-rotation toward Eastern BH for kick serves improves spin leverage while maintaining disguise. Both held by reputable coaches.

### Hybrid: Slice-Kick Second Serve
- Blend of slice and kick; slower than pure slice, more spin
- Unusual air trajectory makes it hard to read
- Popularized by Frances Tiafoe at 2025 Australian Open vs. Medvedev

### Speed vs. Spin Tradeoff
- Flat: highest speed, lowest spin
- Slice: moderate speed, moderate sidespin
- Kick: lowest speed, highest topspin
- Pro benchmark: 1st serve at ~90% max velocity; 2nd serve at ~75% of 1st serve speed
