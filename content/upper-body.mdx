# Upper Body Mechanics

The upper body is the delivery system of tennis. Your legs and hips generate power, but your shoulders, arms, and hands direct that power into the ball with precision. Understanding how each upper body segment contributes to different shots — and where common mechanical breakdowns occur — is the key to consistent, powerful strokes.

---

## 1. The Shoulder Complex

### Anatomy of the Tennis Shoulder

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body: 180 degrees of flexion, 180 degrees of abduction, and 270+ degrees of combined rotation. This mobility makes it both incredibly versatile and vulnerable to injury.

**Key muscles for tennis:**

| Muscle Group | Tennis Function | Shots Where Critical |
|---|---|---|
| Rotator cuff (SITS) | Stabilizes the humeral head in the socket during dynamic movement | All shots — especially serve, overhead |
| Deltoid (anterior/lateral/posterior) | Arm elevation, horizontal movement | Serve (trophy to contact), forehand follow-through |
| Pectoralis major | Horizontal adduction, internal rotation | Forehand drive, serve acceleration |
| Latissimus dorsi | Arm extension, internal rotation, pulling motions | Serve power, topspin forehand |
| Serratus anterior | Scapular protraction and upward rotation | Reaching for wide balls, serve extension |
| Trapezius (upper/mid/lower) | Scapular elevation, retraction, depression | Backswing preparation, overhead stability |

### Shoulder Turn (Unit Turn)

The unit turn is the foundation of every groundstroke. When you see the ball coming, your shoulders, hips, and racket turn together as a single unit — not sequentially.

**Forehand unit turn:**
- Shoulders rotate 80-100 degrees from the net
- Non-dominant hand stays on the racket throat during the turn
- The turn coils the trunk, storing elastic energy for the forward swing
- Eyes stay on the ball — the head doesn't turn with the body

**Backhand unit turn (two-handed):**
- Shoulders rotate 70-90 degrees from the net
- Both hands stay on the racket throughout
- The non-dominant arm (left for a right-hander) drives the turn
- More compact than the forehand turn

**Backhand unit turn (one-handed):**
- Shoulders rotate 90-110 degrees from the net (more rotation needed)
- Non-dominant hand supports the racket throat, then releases
- Requires greater shoulder flexibility and trunk coil

### The Scapular Platform

Before the arm can accelerate forward, the scapula (shoulder blade) must be stable. Think of it as a platform: if the platform wobbles, the arm loses power and accuracy.

**Common 4.0 error:** The shoulder blade wings out during the forward swing because the serratus anterior and lower trapezius are weak. This causes the arm to "fly open" early, losing rotational power and creating impingement risk.

**Fix:** Scapular retraction and depression drills (wall slides, band pull-aparts, Y-T-W raises — as covered in Training & Drills).

## 2. The Arm Path

### Forehand Arm Path

**Modern forehand (semi-western/western grip):**

1. **Backswing:** Racket drops below the hand as the body turns. The arm is relaxed, hanging naturally. The racket tip points roughly behind you.
2. **Forward swing initiation:** The arm lags behind the body — the body uncoils first, the arm follows. This lag is where the whip effect begins.
3. **Low-to-high swing path:** The racket head drops below the contact point and swings upward at 30-45 degrees. This angle creates topspin.
4. **Contact:** Arm is slightly bent (not locked), contact point is in front of the body by 12-18 inches, racket face is perpendicular to the ground or slightly closed.
5. **Windshield wiper finish:** After contact, the forearm pronates and the racket sweeps across the body. The racket finishes over the opposite shoulder or wraps around the body.

**Key checkpoint — the "slot":** At the end of the backswing, the racket should be in a position where the butt cap points toward the incoming ball. This is the loaded "slot" from which the racket accelerates forward.

### Backhand Arm Path (Two-Handed)

1. **Backswing:** Compact — shorter than the forehand. Both hands stay on the racket. The non-dominant arm pulls the racket back.
2. **Forward swing:** The non-dominant arm pushes through the ball (think of it as a one-handed forehand with the left hand for right-handers). The dominant arm guides.
3. **Contact:** Both arms slightly bent, contact point slightly in front of the front hip. Racket face square or slightly closed.
4. **Follow-through:** Both arms extend through the ball, then the racket rises over the dominant shoulder. Less wrist rotation than the forehand.

**Two-handed advantage:** The second hand adds stability and effective mass, making the shot naturally more consistent. The disadvantage: reduced reach and harder to generate heavy topspin (shorter lever arm).

### Backhand Arm Path (One-Handed)

1. **Backswing:** The non-dominant hand cradles the racket throat, then releases as the body turns. The dominant arm extends behind with the racket tip pointing up.
2. **Forward swing:** The arm extends forward, led by the shoulder. The key is to keep the arm relatively straight through contact.
3. **Contact:** Arm nearly fully extended, contact point further in front than the two-hander. The wrist is laid back (dorsiflexed).
4. **Follow-through:** The arm swings up and across the body, finishing high over the non-dominant shoulder.

**One-handed key insight:** Power comes from body rotation, not the arm. Because the arm is extended (longer lever arm), small body rotations translate to high racket speed. But timing is less forgiving — the contact zone is smaller.

### Volley Arm Path

The volley has no backswing. It's a forward punch.

1. **Preparation:** Racket is in front of the body, slightly above the net. Continental grip.
2. **Step and punch:** Step toward the ball with the opposite foot. The arm moves forward with a firm wrist — no swing.
3. **Contact:** In front of the body, racket face slightly open (angle it down for balls above the net, more open for low volleys).
4. **Follow-through:** Minimal — 6-12 inches past contact. The hand finishes in front of the body.

**The key to volleys is the wrist:** A firm wrist keeps the racket face stable. A floppy wrist causes the racket to twist on contact, producing weak, unpredictable shots.

## 3. The Wrist and Hand

### Grip Pressure

Grip pressure affects everything: racket head speed, shot control, and injury risk.

| Pressure Level | When to Use | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 3-4 / 10 (light) | During rally, backswing, preparation | Allows wrist flexibility, maximizes racket head speed |
| 6-7 / 10 (firm) | At contact, volleys | Stabilizes the racket face, increases effective mass |
| 8-10 / 10 (death grip) | Never | Slows racket head speed, causes tennis elbow, locks the kinetic chain |

**The grip pressure cycle:** Light during preparation → firm at contact → light again during follow-through. This cycle should be automatic, not conscious.

### Wrist Action by Shot

| Shot | Wrist Action | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Forehand (modern) | Lag → snap through contact → pronation | Racket head speed and topspin generation |
| Backhand (2H) | Relatively neutral, both wrists firm | Stability and control |
| Backhand (1H) | Laid back (dorsiflexed) through contact | Maintains racket face angle on extended arm |
| Slice (FH or BH) | Slightly beveled open, firm through contact | Underspin control |
| Volley | Firm, locked | Stability, direction control |
| Serve | Pronation through contact | Flat/slice/kick differentiation |
| Drop shot | Relaxed, absorbs pace | Takes speed off the ball |

### Pronation and Supination

**Pronation** (rotating the forearm so the palm faces down) is critical for:
- Flat serve power (the racket face rotates through the ball)
- Forehand topspin (the "windshield wiper" finish)
- Overhead smash

**Supination** (rotating so the palm faces up) is used for:
- Slice serve (brushing the outside of the ball)
- Slice groundstrokes (racket face stays open)

## 4. The Overhead and Serve (Upper Body Focus)

### Trophy Position (shared by serve and overhead)

The trophy position is identical for serves and overheads:
- Elbow at shoulder height
- Racket tip pointing down behind the back (the "back scratch" position)
- Non-dominant arm pointing up at the ball
- Shoulder externally rotated to maximum comfortable range

### The Throwing Motion

The serve and overhead use the same upper body pattern as a baseball throw:
1. External rotation loads the shoulder (cocking phase)
2. Internal rotation accelerates the arm forward (acceleration phase)
3. Pronation rotates the racket through contact
4. The arm decelerates across the body (follow-through)

**Coaching cue:** If you can throw a ball overhead, you can serve. The upper body motion is identical — the racket just extends the lever arm.

## 5. Common Upper Body Errors at 4.0

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Arm-only" forehand | No shoulder turn, hitting with just the arm | Exaggerate the unit turn; point the butt cap at the ball |
| Late contact on backhand | Backswing too long, body doesn't turn enough | Shorten backswing; turn earlier; contact in front of the hip |
| Wristy volleys | Using wrist instead of step-and-punch | Freeze the wrist; step into the ball for power |
| Slice that floats up | Racket face too open, chopping down | Keep the racket face angle constant; swing forward, not down |
| Serve without power | No shoulder rotation, arm pushes the ball | Practice the throwing motion without a racket first |
| Forehand that goes long | Racket face open at contact, no topspin | Low-to-high swing path; windshield wiper finish |
| Elbow pain | Grip too tight, hitting late, excessive wrist action | Lighter grip, hit in front, strengthen forearm extensors |
