# Practice Planning: Structuring Your Development

Improvement in tennis doesn't come from playing more matches or hitting more balls — it comes from *deliberate practice* with clear objectives. Most 4.0 players practice by showing up and hitting. Most 4.5 players practice with a plan: what to work on, how long, at what intensity, and how to measure progress. This section gives you the framework to structure your tennis development week by week.

---

## 1. The Four Types of Practice

### Technical Practice (Skill Building)

**Purpose:** Improve specific strokes or mechanics.
**Intensity:** Low to moderate (60-70% effort). Focus on form, not outcome.
**Example:** Working on topspin forehand swing path, serve toss consistency, or volley footwork.

**Rules:**
- Isolate one element per session (don't try to fix everything)
- Accept errors — you're rewiring motor patterns, not competing
- Use shadow swings between reps to reinforce the new pattern
- 50-80 balls focused on one element is more valuable than 200 balls with no focus

### Tactical Practice (Pattern Building)

**Purpose:** Build point construction patterns and decision-making.
**Intensity:** Moderate (70-80% effort). Execute patterns with intent.
**Example:** Practicing the serve-plus-one pattern, cross-court rally-then-attack, or approach shot sequences.

**Rules:**
- Work specific patterns (not just "play points")
- Use cooperative feeding before live ball (partner feeds to create the situation)
- Repeat the pattern until it feels automatic, then add variability

### Match Play Practice (Competition Simulation)

**Purpose:** Test skills under pressure with scoring.
**Intensity:** High (85-95% effort). Play to win, but with tactical awareness.
**Example:** Practice sets, tiebreaks, first-to-21 games, situation play (start at 3-5 down).

**Rules:**
- Play with a tactical plan (not just swinging)
- After every set, identify one thing that worked and one thing to improve
- Use this to expose weaknesses that need technical or tactical practice

### Physical Practice (Conditioning/Fitness)

**Purpose:** Build the athletic base that supports everything else.
**Intensity:** Variable (based on the session — see Conditioning page).
**Example:** Footwork drills, agility work, strength training, cardio.

## 2. The Weekly Template

### 3 Days Per Week (Minimum for Improvement)

| Day | Type | Duration | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Technical + Tactical | 90 min | 30 min technique drill → 30 min pattern practice → 30 min point play |
| Day 2 | Physical | 45-60 min | Strength training OR conditioning + footwork drills |
| Day 3 | Match Play | 90-120 min | Practice set or competitive match with tactical focus |

### 4 Days Per Week (Good Progress)

| Day | Type | Duration | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Technical | 60-75 min | One stroke focus + serve practice |
| Day 2 | Physical + Footwork | 45-60 min | Strength training + agility ladder + cone drills |
| Day 3 | Tactical + Points | 90 min | Pattern drills → live point play with scoring |
| Day 4 | Match Play | 90-120 min | Match or practice set |

### 5 Days Per Week (Accelerated Development)

| Day | Type | Duration | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | Technical | 60-75 min | Forehand or backhand focus + serve |
| Tuesday | Physical | 45-60 min | Strength + conditioning |
| Wednesday | Tactical | 75-90 min | Pattern practice + situational points |
| Thursday | Physical (light) + Serve | 45 min | Footwork drills + 30 min dedicated serve session |
| Friday | Match Play | 90-120 min | Competitive match or practice set |
| Weekend | Active recovery OR bonus match | Variable | Light hit, swim, or casual doubles |

## 3. The 12-Week Development Cycle

### Weeks 1-4: Assessment and Foundation

**Goal:** Identify your weaknesses and build a practice baseline.

**Week 1 actions:**
- Film yourself hitting forehands, backhands, serves, and volleys (phone on a tripod behind the baseline)
- Play a match and note where you lose points (unforced errors by shot type, patterns that beat you)
- Take the self-assessment from the "Self-Assessment" page

**Weeks 2-4 focus:**
- Identify your top 2 weaknesses (e.g., "backhand under pressure" and "second serve consistency")
- Dedicate 60% of technical practice to these areas
- Establish your weekly routine from the templates above
- Track your first-serve percentage and unforced error count in each practice match

### Weeks 5-8: Targeted Improvement

**Goal:** Make measurable progress on identified weaknesses.

**Focus areas (pick 2-3 per 4-week block):**

| Weakness | Practice Focus | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Forehand consistency | Cross-court 20 drill, deep zone rally | Consecutive balls, % landing deep |
| Backhand under pressure | Backhand-only cross-court games, 21-point BH rallies | Error rate per game |
| First serve % | Deliberate serve sessions (50-80 serves with targets) | % in, % to target zones |
| Second serve | Kick serve technique drills, pressure second serves | Double fault rate per set |
| Net game | Approach-split-volley drill sequences | % of approach points won |
| Footwork | Dedicated footwork sessions, split step focus | Self-rating of movement quality |
| Return of serve | Return-specific drill sessions with partner serving | Return depth and direction consistency |
| Mental game | Between-point routine implementation, pressure games | Self-rated composure 1-10 per match |

### Weeks 9-12: Integration and Competition

**Goal:** Integrate improvements into match play under pressure.

- Increase the ratio of match play to technical practice (50/50 instead of 70/30)
- Play competitive matches weekly with tactical focus
- Film one match and compare to your Week 1 footage
- Re-assess: did your identified weaknesses improve? What's the next priority?

## 4. Serve Practice (Standalone Sessions)

The serve is the only shot you fully control — it deserves dedicated practice time, separate from rallying.

### 30-Minute Serve Session

| Block | Time | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Warm-up | 5 min | Shadow serves, arm circles, 10 easy serves |
| Technique focus | 10 min | One element only (toss, leg drive, pronation, or follow-through). 30-40 serves. |
| Target practice | 10 min | 10 serves to each zone (T, body, wide) on both deuce and ad side |
| Pressure serves | 5 min | Simulate 30-40 scores: "This is break point." Hit first serve. If missed, second serve must go in. |

### Serve Targets by Level

| Target | 4.0 Goal | 4.5 Goal |
|---|---|---|
| First serve % | 55-60% | 60-70% |
| First serve to target zone | 30% | 45-50% |
| Double fault rate | 1 per 2-3 games | 1 per 4-5 games |
| Second serve that opponent attacks | Frequent | Rarely (spin and depth prevent attack) |
| Ace or unreturnable serve per set | 1-2 | 3-5 |

## 5. How to Practice with a Partner

### If Your Partner Is at Your Level

- **Cooperative drills first, competitive second:** Warm up with cross-court rallies, then move to pattern drills, then play points
- **Set specific rules for point play:** "Cross-court only," "Must approach on any ball inside the service line," "Serve and volley every point"
- **Take turns feeding:** One person feeds, the other practices. Switch every 10-15 balls.

### If Your Partner Is Better Than You

- **Ask them to feed at controlled pace:** You practice pattern execution; they practice consistency
- **Play points with a handicap:** They start every game at 0-30 or 0-40
- **Focus on your strengths, not their weaknesses:** Use them to test your best shots under pressure

### If Your Partner Is Weaker Than You

- **Use them for target practice:** Direct balls to specific zones; practice placement over power
- **Work on your weaknesses:** Hit only backhands, or practice your net game
- **Play cooperative games:** "Let's see how long we can rally" builds everyone's consistency

### If You Don't Have a Partner

- **Ball machine:** 30-45 min sessions with programmed patterns (random direction, varying speed)
- **Wall practice:** 15-20 min of continuous hitting develops rhythm and consistency
- **Serve practice:** The only shot that doesn't need a partner
- **Shadow tennis:** Full practice routines with footwork, swing, and recovery but no ball
- **Solo footwork drills:** Agility ladder, cone drills, shadow court movement

## 6. Tracking Progress

### What to Track

| Metric | How to Track | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| First serve % | Count in matches | Every match |
| Double fault rate | Count in matches | Every match |
| Unforced errors (FH/BH breakdown) | Tally during practice matches | Weekly |
| Winners (FH/BH breakdown) | Tally during practice matches | Weekly |
| Cross-court 20 streak | Count during drill | Weekly |
| Deep zone % | Count during rally drills | Weekly |
| Match result | W/L and score | Every match |
| Self-rated composure (1-10) | Post-match journal | Every match |

### The Simple Match Journal

After every competitive match, spend 3 minutes writing:

1. **Score and result**
2. **What worked** (1-2 specific things — "my cross-court forehand was consistent," "I held serve well")
3. **What didn't work** (1-2 specific things — "my backhand broke down at 4-all," "I double faulted under pressure")
4. **One thing to practice next session** (derived from what didn't work)
5. **Composure rating (1-10)** (how well did you manage emotions and routines?)

Over 10-20 matches, patterns emerge. If "backhand under pressure" appears 8 times, that's your priority. If "double faulted in big moments" appears repeatedly, you need pressure serve practice.

## 7. Periodization: Thinking in Seasons

### Off-Season (No Tournaments, 8-12 Weeks)

- **Focus:** Technical work, weakness correction, physical conditioning
- **Ratio:** 50% technical, 20% tactical, 10% match play, 20% physical
- **This is when you make changes.** New grips, swing adjustments, strategic shifts — do it now when results don't matter.

### Pre-Season (4-6 Weeks Before Competition Starts)

- **Focus:** Integration, pattern sharpening, match preparation
- **Ratio:** 25% technical, 30% tactical, 30% match play, 15% physical
- **Taper physical training** slightly; increase match play to build competitive sharpness

### In-Season (Competition Period)

- **Focus:** Maintaining skills, tactical execution, recovery
- **Ratio:** 15% technical, 25% tactical, 40% match play, 20% physical (maintenance)
- **Don't change technique during the season.** Play with what you have. Save the technical work for off-season.

### Transition (1-2 Weeks After Season Ends)

- **Focus:** Active recovery, mental reset
- **Play for fun:** Casual doubles, social tennis, hit with friends without structure
- **Reflect:** Review your match journal. What improved? What's the priority for the next cycle?

## 8. The 4.0→4.5 Practice Priorities

If you have limited time and need to prioritize, focus on these in order:

| Priority | Why | Time Per Week |
|---|---|---|
| 1. First serve consistency | The serve is the only shot you fully control — a reliable first serve changes every service game | 30 min x 2 |
| 2. Forehand depth and topspin | Your primary weapon — needs to keep opponents behind the baseline | 20 min x 3 (during rally sessions) |
| 3. Return of serve | You face serves every other game — a reliable return keeps you in games | 15 min x 2 |
| 4. Footwork (split step) | Movement quality affects every single shot | 10 min warm-up x 3 |
| 5. Backhand consistency | Doesn't need to be a weapon — just needs to not be a liability | 15 min x 2 |
| 6. Mental routine | Between-point routine and composure under pressure | Implemented during all match play |

**Total: approximately 4-5 hours per week of structured practice** — achievable for a working adult who plays 3-4 times per week.

<div class="feel-cue">
<strong>What It Feels Like</strong>
When you practice with a plan, improvement accelerates noticeably. After 4-6 weeks of focused work on a specific weakness, you'll feel the difference in matches — the shot that used to break down under pressure starts holding up. After 12 weeks, you'll look back at your match journal and see concrete evidence of progress. The 4.0→4.5 transition typically takes 6-18 months of deliberate practice. Unstructured hitting can take years to cover the same ground — or never get there at all.
